## Course description

This is the capstone course in the BIT major, Decision Support Systems stream. As such, you will be required to exercise many of the skills you have learnt in earlier core BIT (DSS) courses. In this course, you will demonstrate and develop your skills as a professional business analyst.

The course is unlike other BIT undergraduate courses, as it is project-based, not lecture-based. The primary emphasis of this seminar class is on completing a significant team system development project.

## Team project

For the Team Project, you will be responsible for the analysis, design, and implementation of a complete web-based on mobile-app software system. You are expected to use the Agile software development process, with:

- **Stand Ups**: must be submitted every alternate Monday morning during the team project, via Assignments on Canvas. Standup submissions must be received before Monday midnight on Standup days during the team project. Points will be deducted for that week if your Standup Workbook is not submitted via Assignments on Canvas by Monday midnight, every other Monday during the team project.

- **Sprint Retrospectives**: each Thursday during the team project, teams will be able to receive informal, interactive feedback on their current Sprint work-in-progress. Teams must submit (as a group) a Sprint Retrospective Workbook for the current Sprint (1, 2, or 3) and current phase (Design or Implementation), each Thursday during the team project.

  Please use the same workbook throughout the semester, so your cumulative progress is visible. Role Design and Role Implementation rubrics for all roles, complete up to the current sprint and phase, must be submitted with the Retrospective workbook, as evidence of completion of your tasks.

  The Sprint Retrospective workbook should be completed by the Project Manager for your team. If you dispute your project manager’s evaluation of your effort and accomplishments, submit your version of the Sprint Retrospective workbook separately.

- **Sprint Demos**: complete formal Sprint Demo’s must be presented via recorded video, every alternate Friday during the team project: one video per team. Every team member should participate in the video demo (Participation points will be deducted for non-participation).

  Submit your Product Backlog and PowerPoint slides with your video presentation, via Assignments > Sprint X: Video Demo on Canvas. Please include in your Sprint X: Video Demo submission:

  - Filled in Product Backlog workbook (updated to reflect progress to date, and plans)
  - PowerPoint slides for your demo. Please include:
    - As first slide: the name of your client organization and the project objective
    - As second slide: the names and photos of all team members, as well as the team roles of all team members
    - As third slide: a description of the Minimum Viable Product (MVP) you tackled for this sprint, taken from your Product Backlog
    - Slides showing deliverables produced during the sprint, such as ERDs, DFDs, CRUD matrices, low- and high-fidelity web/mobile app page wireframes/mockups, screencaptures of working pages, and screencaptures of reports created (include a descriptive title for each report). Provide informative titles for every slide. For ERDs/DFDs/CRUDs, highlight in yellow background which specific items were the subject of the current sprint.
    - As final slide: a description of the MVP you plan to tackle for the next sprint, taken from your Product Backlog
  - Demo video for current sprint

- **Teammate peer reviews**: must be submitted at the very end of the project, using the Teammate Peer Evaluation Workbook template provided, via Assignments on Canvas.

You will work in a team of no more than 9 members. (If you choose to work in a team with less than 7 members, you will specify the primary role on your team that you would like to use for your Tech Tut and that you would like to be assessed on for your Individual Role Responsibilities rubric).

Each student must assume at least one core role in your team. There should typically be only 1 student in each of the 7 available role types; however, a team may have 2 Business Intelligence Analysts, and may have 2 Developers.

Most core roles have both Design and Implementation Rubrics; however, UX Leads have only Design Rubrics, and Developers have only Implementation Rubrics.

## Project choices

### Option 1: Help Singapore Management University (SMU) develop web-based peer evaluation

Build a web application which will allow students working in team project to do intra-team peer evaluation, based on a set of rubrics. Sample rubrics can be found at Canvas > Files > TEAM PROJECT > CLIENT ORGANIZATIONS > 1. Singapore Management University.

These rubrics can be mapped to the SMU Graduate Learning Outcomes. For each student, peer evaluation will be consolidated across many courses and the student can see a visual dashboard of consolidated achievement (achieved, working towards, needs improvement, etc.).

Your application should:

- Allow the professor to import students and courses
- Allow the professor to create student groups
- Allow the professor to schedule peer evaluations
- Allow the student to complete peer evaluations
- Provide operational reporting for all of the above

Note: customize to SMU needs (branding, courses, semesters, etc.). Projects that include only VT branding/courses/semesters will lose points.

### Option 2: Help the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) keep American consumers safe

Create a web or mobile-app-based system to help the CPSC find and eliminate recalled products illegally offered for sale online (e.g. on eBay or Craigslist). You may use simple text-matching (e.g. manufacturer name and/or model number) to find recall violations.

Project should show multiple phases of incident management:

- Identifying high-priority recalls and/or allowing a CPSC Manager to shortlist a few (3–5 recalls)
- Showing 3rd-party marketplace listings that potentially match a high-priority/short-listed recall
  - Prefer importing via automated API (e.g. eBay API) or web-scrape
  - Manual search + input is permissible
- Annotating listings (true/false positive; capture commentary)
- Contacting seller and/or marketplace about the illegal listing
- Allowing seller/marketplace response
- Adjudicating the response; coding incident status (resolved/unresolved) and resolution reason

#### CPSC sequence of events (reflect in ERD)

Recall (announcement by CPSC) → Prioritization (by CPSC Manager) → Listing (by seller) → Violation (noticed by investigator) → Response (by seller) → Adjudication (by investigator)

Each event has its own actor and date-time stamp (e.g., in Prioritization, ManagerID is the actor; in Violation, InvestigatorID is the actor; etc.).

#### Obtaining CPSC recall data

- Recall API info: `http://www.cpsc.gov/en/Recalls/CPSC-Recalls-Application-Program-Interface-API-Information/`
- All recalls in XML: `http://www.saferproducts.gov/RestWebServices/Recall?format=xml`  
  Wait ~10 minutes for the large page to load; save as `.xml`; import to Excel via Data > From Other Sources > From XML Data Import. (Includes recalls back to ~1976 or earlier.)
- Abbreviated set: `http://www.saferproducts.gov/SPDB.zip`  
  Unzip and open the “Recalls” spreadsheet (includes 6,843 recalls, back to 1972).

## Project guidance and requirements

Keep in mind that the individual contribution is a large portion of your course grade. You must take enough tasks to demonstrate sufficient personal effort and achievement. You must diligently complete and submit your Standup spreadsheet every Monday during the team project.

For project managers: be selective with who you admit; choose members with complementary skills and assign tasks up front.

Your project must:

- Be a web application or mobile app
- Have a database / big data back-end
- Provide visual, graphical charts / dashboards
- Be deployed using a commercial hosting provider at a publicly available URL (not localhost)
- Provide login features so the project is not accessible by the general public
- Be demonstrated in a recorded video (e.g. MP4 or video on YouTube) included with your submission (submit privately via Canvas)

**Prototype disclaimer requirement**: projects are prototypes, not endorsed for public release. Do not post your video publicly. Mark clearly as a student project and not endorsed by the imagined client (SMU or CPSC). Ensure login facilities restrict access for grading only.

You may use your technology of choice: raw code (PHP, Python, Java), frameworks (Bootstrap, Joomla, Drupal, CodeIgniter, Ionic, etc.), or drag-and-drop (Mendix, Zoho Creator, WordPress, Bubble, Corona, etc.). Part of your challenge is selecting appropriate technologies.

Datastores can be relational (MySQL, Amazon RDS, Google BigQuery, etc.) or no-SQL (MongoDB, DynamoDB, etc.).

## Tech Tut

For the Tech Tut, you must:

- Give a 20-minute recorded video presenting your PowerPoint slide deck, and doing a demo
- Submit a 20–40 slide PowerPoint slide deck describing:
  - Who your vendor is
  - How your vendor compares to competitors
  - What features your vendor provides
  - How you would apply your vendor’s features to your project
- Submit a 20–40 page Microsoft Word document with detailed step-by-step instructions and screen-captures to teach others how you applied the vendor to your project

You may use AI-driven documentation generation tools like Folge (`https://folge.me/`) and Scribe (`https://scribe.com/`) to reduce manual effort.

You will be assigned a Tech Tut topic at the start of the semester. Every student must present on a unique vendor (no duplicates). Your Tech Tut must be specifically for your team project; you get no credit for arbitrary work unrelated to the team project.

Tech Tuts allow students to explore vendors and technologies. If you discover a more appropriate technology, you are encouraged to re-implement with the alternative for the final team project.

## Tech Tut peer evaluation

You must complete five (5) Tech Tut peer evaluations — one for each project role type.

- For roles aside from your own: evaluate other members of your own team after their presentations.

Use the Tech Tut peer evaluation workbook provided. The workbook requires ratings and comments about what you learned and how they can improve. You receive credit for both the peer evaluation received of you and how diligently you evaluate your peers.

If you are missing a team member in a certain role (e.g. Process Analyst), evaluate someone in that role in another team.
